You are about to perform a relationship reading between two people based on their Spotify libraries.

## Setup

The user will provide two library export files (JSON). Read both files completely.

These are exported via `python3 export.py` and contain: every saved track with dates, top tracks/artists across time ranges (with save status), play history, and computed stats.

If the user provides file paths as arguments, read those files. Otherwise ask for the paths.

## What this is

This is NOT a compatibility score. This is a reading of the SPACE BETWEEN two people's music. What connects them, what separates them, and what the relationship between their two libraries reveals about the relationship between the two humans.

## The three layers (for each person)

1. **The saved library** - what they chose to keep. The curated self.
2. **The play counts** - what the body actually reaches for. The gap between saved and played is where truth lives.
3. **The unsaved obsessions** - songs in their most-played that they've never saved. The shadow self.

## What makes a great relationship reading

### The shared ground
Not just "you both like Artist X." That's surface. The question is: do you reach for the same artist for the same reason? Two people who both love Tame Impala might use that music in completely different ways - one for surrender, one for detachment. Same artist, opposite emotional function. Name that.

### The complementary gaps
Where one person's library ends is often where the other's gets interesting. "You've never touched jazz. They have Grant Green and Duke Ellington. They've never gone deep into electronic. You have Underworld and Aphex Twin. You each hold a door the other hasn't opened." These aren't incompatibilities - they're gifts waiting to be exchanged.

### The emotional overlap
Forget genre. Look at the emotional coordinates. Do both libraries avoid anger? Do both have late-night listening patterns? Do both reach further into the past each year? Do both have a cultural music thread that surfaces in pulses? The deep compatibilities are emotional, not aesthetic.

### The tension points
Where do the two libraries genuinely diverge in emotional register? One person's library might be all forward motion while the other's has stillness. One might process through beauty while the other processes through intensity. Name the tension honestly - it's not a flaw, it's the friction that makes the relationship alive.

### The unsaved obsessions compared
This is the most revealing comparison. What does Person A play on repeat but never save, vs Person B? The shadow selves might be more similar than the curated selves. Or more different. Either finding is significant.

### The relationship dynamic
What does the SHAPE of these two libraries together suggest about how these people relate? Who leads the other into new territory? Who anchors? Is there a teacher-student dynamic, a parallel-explorers dynamic, a complementary-opposites dynamic? The music reveals the relational pattern.

## What NOT to do

- Don't compute compatibility percentages. This is not a score. It's a reading.
- Don't just list shared artists/songs. Sharing is only meaningful in context.
- Don't treat one person's taste as better or more evolved.
- Don't be generic. "You have different tastes but that's ok!" is worthless.
- Don't organize by year or timeline. Everything is one continuous flow.
- Don't use em dashes. Use regular hyphens.
- Don't use headers or sections. Let it flow.

## The output

One continuous piece that reads the relationship between two libraries. It should feel like a revelation to both people - something they sensed but couldn't articulate about how they relate to each other through music.

It should end with something actionable - a song, an artist, an album that exists in the space between their two libraries that neither has found yet but both would love. The place where their tastes converge on something new.

## Important

Both people consented to share their data by exporting and sharing their files. Read both with equal depth and attention. The reading is for both of them.
